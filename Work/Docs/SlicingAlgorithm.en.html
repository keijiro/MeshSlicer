MESHSLICER / RUNTIME — ALGORITHM NOTES

How Plane-Based Mesh Slicing Works

From triangle classification and clipping to cap generation

MeshSlicer is a library that cuts a watertight (2-manifold) mesh with an infinite plane and returns two closed meshes, each sealed with a cap over the cut cross section. This document walks through what Slicer.Slice(Mesh, Plane) does internally, following the order of the pipeline.

Covered code: Assets/MeshSlicer/Runtime/ (Slicer.cs / Vertex.cs / CapBuilder.cs / PolygonTriangulator.cs and friends). The Burst-accelerated implementation (BurstSlicer.cs) is out of scope here.

1Input and Output

The input is a Mesh to cut and a plane (normal n and distance d) defined in the mesh's local space. The output is a SliceResult holding two meshes: Positive, the piece on the side the normal points to, and Negative, the piece on the other side. If the whole mesh lies on one side, the other piece is null.

2Pipeline Overview

The whole process runs in six stages:

→
2Probe & offset the cut position

→

→
5Chain loops & triangulate
→
6Add caps to both pieces

Stages 3–6 are the core: cut every triangle against the plane and seal the openings. Stage 2 — the "offset" — is a preprocessing step that avoids degenerate cases where the plane passes exactly through vertices or edges; it is covered in detail in Section 6.

3Classifying Vertices by Signed Distance

First, every vertex gets a signed distance to the plane, d = dot(n, p) + plane.d: positive means the normal side, negative the other side. The raw value is not used directly, though — a vertex very close to the plane (within a tolerance ε) has its distance snapped to 0 and is treated as lying on the plane. 

static float SignedDist(float3 p, float3 n, float d, float eps)
{
    var s = math.dot(n, p) + d;
    return math.abs(s) <= eps ? 0f : s;   // snap near-plane vertices onto the plane
}

ε scales with the model (ε = max(largest bounding-box dimension × 10⁻⁴, 10⁻⁶)). It keeps vertices that would otherwise flip between "barely positive" and "barely negative" due to floating-point error consistently classified as on-plane.

Each triangle is then sorted into one of three cases by the signs of its three vertices:

  • All d ≥ 0 — copied straight to the Positive piece
  • All d ≤ 0 — copied straight to the Negative piece
  • Mixed positive and negative — the triangle straddles the plane and gets split (next section)
  • All three d = 0 (a face coplanar with the plane) — a zero-thickness degenerate face, discarded

4Splitting Straddling Triangles

A straddling triangle is clipped against each of the two half-spaces using the Sutherland–Hodgman algorithm. Walking the three edges in order with the rule "output inside vertices as-is; insert an intersection point where an edge crosses the plane" yields a convex polygon on each side — either a triangle or a quad. A quad is then fanned into two triangles.

One important detail: vertices with d = 0 (on the plane) are included in both output polygons. This is what lets a triangle with a vertex sitting on the plane split cleanly into two pieces with no gap.

Interpolating vertex attributes at intersections

The intersection point along an edge comes from the ratio of the two signed distances, t = dₐ / (dₐ − d_b). The new vertex interpolates not just position but also the normal, tangent, and UV with the same t (Vertex.Lerp). Interpolated normals and tangents lose unit length, so they are renormalized. This keeps shading and texturing continuous with the original mesh across the cut.

5Building the Cap (Cut Cross Section)

The steps so far produce the "walls" of each piece, but the cut faces are still open. The cut segments gathered during splitting (the thick segments in Figs. 2 and 3) are used to reconstruct the cross-section outline and seal it. CapBuilder does this in three stages:

① Welding endpoints and removing duplicates

Segments generated independently by each triangle have endpoints that differ by tiny floating-point errors. Coordinates are therefore quantized to a grid around 10⁻⁵ of the model size, and endpoints falling into the same cell are welded into one point. Also, an edge lying exactly on the plane is reported as the same segment by both of its adjacent triangles, so duplicate segments are dropped.

② Chaining into closed loops

After welding, the segments share endpoints, so they can be chained end-to-end into closed loops. The cross section of a watertight mesh cut by a plane is always one or more closed contours — an outer boundary, possibly with holes.

③ 2D projection and triangulation

Loop points are projected onto two in-plane axes (u, v) to get 2D polygons (they are also normalized to unit size as a numeric-robustness measure). The actual triangulation is handled by PolygonTriangulator:

  • Outer contours vs. holes — for each loop, count how many other loops contain it: an even count means a solid outer contour, an odd count means a hole (the even-odd rule). This correctly handles doughnut-shaped and nested cross sections.
  • Hole bridging — each hole loop is joined to its outer contour through a bridge edge between a mutually visible pair of vertices, turning the region into a single hole-free polygon (the purple edge in Fig. 5 ③; it is inserted as a doubled there-and-back edge).
  • Ear clipping — the classic method of repeatedly cutting off convex corners triangulates the resulting polygon.

Adding the cap to both pieces

The resulting triangles are added to both pieces with the same shape but opposite orientations. Seen from the Positive piece, the cut faces away from the plane normal, so its cap uses normal −n with reversed winding; the Negative piece keeps normal +n and the original winding (see Fig. 1). Cap vertices get a tangent built from an in-plane axis and UV = (0, 0).

6Offsetting the Cut Plane — Avoiding Degeneracy

Everything above works as long as the plane crosses triangle edges cleanly. Things change when the plane passes exactly through a vertex or along an edge: the intersection collapses to a point, or the cut segments end up interrupted or branching, and the cut boundary no longer forms closed loops. With a broken boundary the triangulation falls apart and the cap comes out damaged — typically with holes in it.

MeshSlicer therefore probes the cut position before doing the real slice. The probe runs only the lightweight segment-collection pass (no clipping, no mesh building) and inspects the welded boundary graph. The test is simple: every point must have degree exactly 2 (i.e., exactly two segments meet there) — which is equivalent to the boundary being a union of simple closed loops.

When the probe fails, the plane is shifted by m × ε along its normal and probed again. The candidate multipliers m are listed below — starting at 0 and alternating to both sides with escalating steps: 

// Nudge amounts (in units of ε) tried when the cut position is degenerate.
// Escalating and two-sided.
static readonly float[] NudgeSteps = { 0, 1, -1, 3, -3, 8, -8, 24, -24, 72, -72, 216, -216 };

Even the largest offset, 216ε, is only about 2% of the model size, so the change in cut position is practically invisible. Most cases resolve within the first step or two. The probe is a light O(triangle count) pass that only collects segments, so it adds very little cost to a normal, non-degenerate slice. And because the probe and the real slice share the intersection code (TryCutSegment), the boundary that passed the probe is exactly the boundary produced by the actual cut. If every candidate fails, the slicer falls back to a best-effort cut at the original position.

7Summary — Mapping to the Source

In short: MeshSlicer sorts triangles by signed distance, clips only the straddling ones, then assembles the cut segments into closed loops and seals them with caps. Three layers of defense guard against numeric error: snapping near-plane vertices to zero (ε), quantized welding of segment endpoints, and the offset search for the cut position itself.

FileRole
Slicer.cs Entry point: distance computation, offset search, triangle classification and clipping, overall assembly
Vertex.cs A vertex with position / normal / tangent / UV and its linear interpolation (Lerp)
CapBuilder.cs Collecting, welding, and chaining cut segments; 2D projection; the boundary-graph degree check
PolygonTriangulator.cs Triangulation of polygons with holes (outer/hole classification, bridging, ear clipping)
MeshBuilder.cs Accumulates triangles and bakes them into a Mesh
SliceResult.cs The result (the Positive / Negative mesh pair)

The input is assumed to be a watertight (2-manifold) mesh. Open meshes or self-intersecting meshes may produce cut boundaries that do not close into loops, in which case the caps cannot be built correctly (the wall splitting itself still works).
